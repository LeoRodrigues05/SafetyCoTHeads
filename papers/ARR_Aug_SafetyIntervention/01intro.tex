\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Modern Large Language Models (LLMs) are post-trained to follow benign instructions while refusing harmful ones, using instruction tuning and preference based optimization \citep{ouyang2022training,bai2022constitutional}. Yet, jailbreak attacks can elicit harmful responses from otherwise safety trained models \citep{wei2023jailbroken,zou2023universal}. In parallel, mechanistic interpretability has developed \emph{white-box interventions} that alter model internals, for example, by ablation of safety-relevant attention heads or neurons, removing refusal directions, or steering activations \citep{zhou2024ships, zhao2025neuron, arditi2024refusal}. These interventions are often presented not merely as ways to change behavior but as evidence of where a model's safety behavior lies.

The usual evidence for such a claim is an increase in Attack Success Rate (ASR) after the intervention. Benchmarks such as HarmBench and JailbreakBench standardize output-level attack evaluation \citep{mazeika2024harmbench,chao2024jailbreakbench}, but ASR alone cannot account for three explanations. First, a model may already have a high ASR pre-intervention. Second, an intervention may increase the attack rate by degrading the coherence of responses rather than selectively removing safety behavior; StrongREJECT documents this failure mode for jailbreak evaluation \citep{souly2024strongreject}. Third, response level refusal is not equivalent to recognizing that a request is harmful; harmfulness and refusal can be represented separately, and some attacks suppress refusal while preserving a model's harmfulness judgment \citep{zhao2025harmfulnessrefusal}. For models that expose a chain-of-thought (CoT), the visible trace provides an additional view of this distinction \citep{baker2025cotmonitoring,korbak2025monitorability,turpin2023unfaithful}. Evidence for selective removal should therefore account for the change induced relative to baseline, the preservation of output quality, and consistency between the visible reasoning and final response.  

We operationalize this behavioral signature with the \textbf{Selective Failure Score} (SFS), a composite, decomposable metric with three baseline-corrected axes: \textbf{Potency}, the coherent harm induced by the intervention; \textbf{Coherence Retention}, the fraction of coherent responses preserved post-intervention; and \textbf{Trace-Response Consistency}, whether the reasoning trace remains predictive of final response harmfulness. Following multi-metric evaluation practice \citep{liang2023helm}, we report the three-axis vector as the primary result and combine it with a geometric mean for a headline result. SFS evaluates the behavioral evidence for a localization claim; a high score is necessary but not sufficient to establish that a particular internal component is the mechanism response for safety. 

We evaluate four intervention families, attention-head ablation, neuron ablation, activation steering, and refusal-direction ablation, in one controlled grid spanning five model/configurations (three of which are safety trained) and two harmful query datasets. Our main contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item We introduce SFS and a shared evaluation protocol that separates intervention-induced harm from baseline harm. Raw coherence-filtered ASR and SFS produce non-trivially different rankings, with Kendall's $\tau$ falling to $0.73$ in our grid. 
    \item On our safety trained models, direction-based methods provide stronger behavioral evidence of failure on average (SFS $0.67$ for steering and $0.47$ for directional ablation) relative to neuron or head ablation ($0.26$ and $0.23$), although no intervention outperforms the others in every model.
    \item We fine-tune a twelve-label pathway judge and validate it against human annotations. On models with explicit reasoning traces, interventions that induce harm generally preserve visible harm recognition and refusal initiation but increase the downstream execution of harmful queries. This pattern is consistent with bypassing the effect of safety reasoning on the answer rather than erasing safety recognition itself. 
\end{enumerate}
