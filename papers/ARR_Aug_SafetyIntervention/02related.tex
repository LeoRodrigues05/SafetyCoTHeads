\paragraph{Safety evaluation}
Safety behavior in LLMs is commonly evaluated against specific benchmarks. HarmBench \citep{mazeika2024harmbench} and JailbreakBench \citep{chao2024jailbreakbench} report ASR using LLM-as-a-judge to classify whether a response complies with the harmful query. StrongReject \citep{souly2024strongreject} also uses an LLM to classify compliance, but additionally asks to score whether the response is specific and convincing, and reports a metric composed of the 3. Such composite metrics are in line with \citep{liang2023helm}, which argues that LLMs behavior consists of several characteristics and that LLMs should not be evaluated on a single axis. 
\\
While these benchmarks evaluate safety behavior in general, our metric measures the effect of interventions, comparing responses before and after.
Inspired by StrongReject, our metric reflects harmfulness and quality of responses but adds monitorability as a factor, reflecting whether the potential harm is verbalized in reasoning. We report the vector along the 3 axes with a Pareto front and additionally define how these axes can be combined into a single number

\paragraph{White-box intervention methods}
Several methods investigate LLM safety by intervening on model parameters. Specifically, white-box methods control which parameters to change and therefore localize safety-mechanisms, attributing the change in safety behavior to the chosen parameters. \citep{zhou2024ships} locate safety-mechanisms in attention heads, \citep{zhao2025neuron} and \citep{yu2026safeseek} claim to find safety-mechanisms as a collection of neurons from attention and MLP-layers, \citep{arditi2024refusal} attribute safety behavior to vectors in the activation space, while \citep{li2025layers} identify layers of the LLM responsible for safety behavior. 

\paragraph{Intervention evaluation}
It is common practice to support the identification of safety-mechanisms by reporting ASR as the single measurement. \citep{zhao2025neuron} reports ASR using the generation of specific harmful responses as a success criterion, following \citep{zou2023universal}.
\citep{zhou2024ships} reports ASR using the absence of refusal keywords as a success criterion. \citet{arditi2024refusal} also reports this but, in addition, it reports a score using an LLM to address potential incoherent responses. \citep{yu2026safeseek} reports ASR using  LlamaGuard3-8B to classify whether an attack is successful.
\\
This shows that there is no standard way to report how safety behavior is affected by interventions. In addition, reporting ASR overstates the harm caused by interventions, as incoherent responses count as harmful, there is no baseline-correction, and reasoning about safety is ignored. We propose a standard protocol that faithfully measures how ASR is affected by an intervention, which is reflected in the Potency-axis. Furthermore, our metric also encapsulates whether response-quality and reasoning are affected.

\paragraph{CoT-monitoring}
\citep{baker2025cotmonitoring} shows that an LLM can be used to monitor the CoT for signals of harmful behavior that may not be caught in the answer. In the same vein, \citep{korbak2025monitorability} mentions that reasoning LLMs use CoT in the same way as activations and argues that monitoring reasoning is a valuable additional safety layer.

With the Safety-Reasoning axis, we address whether an intervention affects harmful signals that can be monitored in the CoT.
