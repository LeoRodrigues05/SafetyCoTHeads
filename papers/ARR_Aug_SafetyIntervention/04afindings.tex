\section{Do Interventions Produce Selective Safety Failures?}
\label{sec:compare}

% A white-box intervention that claims to isolate a model's safety behavior is essentially claiming that \emph{the components targeted by the intervention are responsible for the refusal of harmful requests}. We argue that a single metric is incapable of providing insight into whether an intervention effectively captures the \emph{safety mechanism} of a model. We then ask whether the intervention's effect has the \emph{behavioral signature} of a removal of the safety mechanism, along the three axes of \S\ref{sec:axes}. It should make the model produce coherent harmful answers that were previously refused/not harmful (potency, $\Ppot$); it should degrade safety reasoning without degrading the model's general competence (selectivity, $\Qual$); and it should leave the model's safety reasoning otherwise intact such that chain-of-thought still serves as a marker for harm (monitorability, $\Sreason$).

This section establishes, (i) raw-ASR systematically overstates how well interventions localize safety (\S\ref{subsec:c1}), and (ii)~once the measure is corrected, the families separate sharply, by intervention method and by model type, into faithful and unfaithful representations of the safety mechanism. Our experiments in the \model{OLMo3-base} models serve as a metric of how well the interventions work when there's little mechanism for discovery.

Intervention-induced behavior is necessary but not a sufficient condition for the mechanization localization claim. We therefore verify if each intervention produces the selective failure signal defined in \S\ref{sec:axes}, reporting the $(\Ppot,\Qual,\Sreason)$ vector and using $\SFS$ as a summary. These quantities provide behavioral evidence consistent with localization; however, they do not provide evidence that targeted components capture the safety mechanism behind the model. 

We run our experiments on three safety-trained variants: \model{Qwen3-8B} \citep{yang2025qwen3}, \model{Llama-3.1-8B} \citep{grattafiori2024llama3}, and \model{OLMo-3-Think} \citep{teamolmo2025olmo3}.

\subsection{Raw Attack Success Rate Conflates Baseline Harm}
\label{subsec:c1}

StrongREJECT evaluations already show that apparent attack success is not a fair measure when the response is of low-quality or generally unhelpful \citep{souly2024strongreject}. White-box intervention has an additional problem: even a coherence-filtered harmful-answer rate $\HAC$ does not identify how much harm was induced above the model's baseline; nor does $HAC$ report how much coherence or trace monitorability the intervention retained. 

We first ask whether raw $\HAC$ and $\SFS$ order interventions similarly. For each model arm, we macro-average the two dataset-level values for each of the ten
non-baseline conditions and compute Kendall's $\tau_b$, which accounts for ties \citep{kendall1945ties}. Agreement is high on the safety-trained arms:
$\tau_b(\HAC,\SFS)=0.91$ on \model{Qwen3-8B}, $0.91$ on \model{Llama-3.1-8B}, and $1.00$ on \model{OLMo-3-Think}. The two pre-alignment controls differ: agreement is $0.73$ for transfer and $0.94$ for self. Replacing raw $\HAC$ with baseline-corrected Potency yields $\tau_b(\Ppot,\SFS)=0.82$--$1.00$ across all five arms, including $0.82$ on the transfer control. Raw $\HAC$ is therefore not uniformly uninformative; its comparability breaks most clearly when harmful behavior is already present at baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:raw-vs-sfs} shows the corresponding cell-level relationship across the primary grid.

\paragraph{The failure mode is baseline harm.}
The OLMo-3 base model makes the failure mode concrete. On JailbreakBench, in the no-intervention cell, the model exhibits a higher $\HAC$ score at $66\%$. After intervention, raw $\HAC$ for all ten non-baseline conditions remains relatively high ($0.65$--$0.81$), indicating successful interventions. Baseline corrected scores $\Ppot$ indicate that this higher score is largely inherited from the base model and not intervention specific. This is visualized in Figure \ref{fig:baseline}.

\subsection{Safety-Trained Models Exhibit Distinct Selective-Failure Signatures}
\label{subsec:c2}

The substantive comparison is across the three safety-trained arms. Their responses are not variations of a single family leaderboard: the same intervention family can produce a strong selective-failure signature on one checkpoint and remain nearly inert on another (Figure~\ref{fig:family-model}).

\paragraph{\model{Qwen3-8B}: direction-level interventions dominate.}
Directional ablation and activation steering attain mean $\SFS$ values of $0.58$ and $0.49$, respectively, whereas SHIPS and neuron ablation remain near
$0.13$ and $0.12$. At the strongest steering dose, however, steering reaches $\SFS=0.61$, comparable to directional ablation. The supported distinction is
therefore not that directional ablation uniformly outperforms steering, but that direction-level interventions produce substantially stronger selective
failure than the tested sparse head and neuron selectors on this model.

\paragraph{\model{Llama-3.1-8B}: broad susceptibility, but different diagnoses.}
Steering is strongest on average ($\SFS=0.88$), while directional ablation ($0.58$) and neuron ablation ($0.51$) also have substantial effects. The neuron
result is concentrated at the largest budget, where \condition{neurons\_top1024} reaches $\Ppot=0.41$. SHIPS obtains a lower mean score ($0.30$) and does so with pronounced collateral degradation: on JailbreakBench, \condition{ships\_top5} and \condition{ships\_top8} reduce Coherence Retention to $\Qual=0.44$ and $0.46$. By contrast, steering averages $\Ppot=0.78$ with $\Qual=0.93$. The decomposition, therefore, distinguishes selectively induced harm from an intervention that partly breaks the model.

\paragraph{\model{OLMo-3-Think}: vulnerability is selector-specific.}
Activation steering reaches a mean $\SFS$ of $0.64$ and rises to $0.88$ at $\alpha=1.5$, where $\Ppot=0.77$ and $\Qual=0.99$. Directional ablation, SHIPS,
and neuron ablation remain between $0.14$ and $0.27$. Low scores for the component-level methods should therefore not be interpreted as global resistance of the checkpoint: the same model is strongly affected by activation addition along the discovered direction.

\paragraph{Direction-based methods are strongest on average, not universally.}
Pooling the three safety-trained arms, activation steering has the largest mean $\SFS$ ($0.67$), followed by directional ablation ($0.47$), neuron ablation
($0.26$), and SHIPS head ablation ($0.23$; Table~\ref{tab:intervention_family}). The separation is driven primarily by Potency: mean Coherence Retention and Monitorability Retention remain at least $0.88$ for every family. Under the tested target-selection procedures and intervention strengths, direction-based methods, therefore, provide stronger behavioral evidence for localized control of refusal. This does not establish that safety ``is a direction,'' nor that the selected heads or neurons are irrelevant; the methods operate at different representational levels, and their strength scales are not commensurate.

\paragraph{Coherence Retention changes the diagnosis.}
Coherence-gating ensures that Potency is computed only among usable responses, while $\Qual$ separately reports how many responses survive that gate. This
distinction is clearest for SHIPS on \model{Llama-3.1-8B}: the apparent answer-level effect of larger head ablations is accompanied by severe coherence loss, whereas steering induces substantially more harm while preserving model behavior. Thus, two interventions with similar apparent attack success need not provide equally strong evidence for selective safety removal.

Taken together, the decomposition changes the diagnosis in three ways. Baseline correction separates induced harm from inherited harm; Coherence Retention separates selective failure from collateral degradation; and the remaining effects are strongly method-, model-, and dose-dependent. No intervention family produces a uniformly strong selective-failure signature across the primary grid.
