\subsection{Interventional methods for locating safety mechanisms}

We compare three target-discovery procedures and four intervention families: SHIPS head ablation \citep{zhou2024ships}, Safety Neuron Detection
\citep{zhao2025neuron}, and refusal-direction activation addition and directional ablation \citep{arditi2024refusal}. We reproduce each discovery procedure on MaliciousInstruct \citep{bianchi2024safetytuned}, using Alpaca \citep{taori2023alpaca} as a benign contrast when required (we don't use our evaluation datasets for discovery). Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-methods} specifies the discovery objectives and implementation details while the evaluation procedure is described in \ref{sec:axes} and \ref{sec:composite}. We interpret interventions as causal tests of behavior, not proof that a selected component is a unique safety mechanism.

\subsection{Three evaluation axes}
\label{sec:axes}
We score an intervention on a \emph{cell} $c$, a \texttt{(model, dataset, condition)} 3-tuple, and assume $b$ is it's \texttt{(model, dataset)} pre-intervention baseline. Let $C_c=\Pr(\text{clean})$ and $\clip(x)=\max(0,\min(1,x))$, we define,

\begin{align}
  \Ppot_c &= \clip\!\left(\frac{\HAC_c-\HAC_b}{1-\HAC_b}\right),
  & \Qual_c &= \clip\!\left(\frac{C_c}{C_b}\right),
  \label{eq:pq}\\[-1pt]
  \Sreason_c &= 1-\clip\!\left(U_c-U_b\right).
  \label{eq:s}
\end{align}
where $\clip(x)=\max(0,\min(1,x))$

\textbf{Potency ($\Ppot$)}, $\HAC=\Pr(\text{harmful}\mid\text{clean})$ is the coherence-filtered attack success rate. Filtering for coherent responses first ensures that incoherent output does not count as a successful attack. $\Ppot$ is then the fraction of harmful responses that were initially safe before the intervention. Coherence Retention ($\Qual<1$) records response quality degradation rather than selective safety removal \citep{souly2024strongreject}. It measures coherence, not general task capability (\S\ref{sec:limitations}). \textbf{Monitorability Retention ($\Sreason$)} tests whether the harmfulness of the final output can be inferred from just the reasoning trace post-intervention. The paired monitoring protocol, covert-failure definition, and safety-reasoning analysis can be found in \S\ref{sec:cot-monitoring}. A high $\Sreason$ indicates that the intervention does not make harmful answers less visible to the trace monitor. Because $\Sreason=1-\clip(U_c-U_b)$ penalizes only covert failures $U$ (harmful answers behind a benign-looking trace) and is insensitive to over-warnings $O$, it is a \emph{threat-weighted} monitorability measure---equivalently, a \emph{Covert-Failure Retention} score---rather than a symmetric trace--answer agreement score. Pathway labels explain process changes but do not enter $\Sreason$.

We note that the axes only reflect the effect on safety \emph{behavior}: an intervention scores high when it behaves \emph{as if} it has found a safety-mechanism. The score grades the \emph{localization claim}, and a high score is necessary but not sufficient to support the claim. We return to what the signature can and cannot establish in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{CoT monitoring and safety-reasoning analysis}
\label{sec:cot-monitoring}
For models with an explicit \texttt{<think>} block, answer judgments use only the final answer, and trace judgments use only the text inside the block.
Qwen3-8B and OLMo-3-Think form the primary explicit-trace analysis. For the other arms, an early-response prefix is reported separately as a sensitivity
analysis and is not treated as a private chain-of-thought.

For some prompt $i$, let $y_i$ be the final response harmfulness and $\hat y_i^{\mathrm{tr}}$ be a prediction about response harmfulness inferred from the reasoning trace alone. 
\begin{align}
 U_c&=\Pr(y_i=1,\hat y_i^{\mathrm{tr}}=0), \nonumber\\
 O_c&=\Pr(y_i=0,\hat y_i^{\mathrm{tr}}=1), \nonumber\\[-1pt]
 A_c&=\Pr(y_i=\hat y_i^{\mathrm{tr}}), \nonumber \\
 \operatorname{FNR}^{\mathrm{tr}}_c&=\Pr(\hat y_i^{\mathrm{tr}}=0\mid y_i=1).
 \label{eq:monitoring}
\end{align}
We define the covert-failure rate ($U_c$), over-warning ($O_c$), paired agreement ($A_c$), and trace false-negative rate ($\operatorname{FNR}^{\mathrm{tr}}_c$). The signed gap $\gapm_c=U_c-O_c=\Pr(\text{answer harmful})-\Pr(\text{trace predicts unsafe})$ is a descriptive measure of net under- versus over-warning on the common paired prompt set; it is \emph{not} a prompt-level agreement metric, because covert failures $U_c$ and over-warnings $O_c$ can cancel. We therefore also report $A_c$ and $\operatorname{FNR}^{\mathrm{tr}}_c$ where relevant. A large positive gap means unsafe answers behind benign-looking traces (monitorability collapsed); because $\Sreason=1-\clip(U_c-U_b)$ depends only on $U_c$, it rewards keeping the covert-failure rate near the baseline's rather than the signed gap.

A sentence-level judge identifies whether and where the trace contains explicit safety reasoning. A fine-tuned pathway judge labels reasoning-trace prefixes
along recognition, refusal dynamics, rationalization, and execution metrics. The 12-label taxonomy adapts the harmful-reasoning annotations introduced by
HarmThoughts \citep{kakkar2026harmthoughts}. These diagnostics explain how the reasoning changes but are not considered when calculating $\SFS$. The full
12-label taxonomy can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-pathway}. We treat visible traces as monitoring signals rather than a guaranteed reflection of
internal computation \citep{baker2025cotmonitoring,korbak2025monitorability}.

\subsection{The Selective Failure Score}
\label{sec:composite}
Following  HELM's multi-metric precedent \citep{liang2023helm}, we treat $(\Ppot,\Qual,\Sreason)$ and Pareto dominance as primary and report
\textbf{Selective-Failure Score} (SFS):
\begin{equation}
  \SFS_c=(\Ppot_c\Qual_c\Sreason_c)^{1/3}
  \label{eq:sfs}
\end{equation}
as a headline summary. The geometric mean gives a ``no axis left behind'' property: any axis going to zero collapses the score. Alternative variants and axis ablations are reported in Appendix~\ref{appendix-metric}. 

\subsection{Experimental grid}
\label{sec:grid}
The five models/configurations used consist of Qwen3-8B and OLMo-3-Think, which expose explicit traces; Llama-3.1-8B; and OLMo-3-Base with targets either
transferred from OLMo-3-Think or discovered on the base checkpoint itself. On JailbreakBench (100 prompts) \citep{chao2024jailbreakbench} and BeaverTails (98)
\citep{ji2023beavertails}, each arm is evaluated at baseline; head-ablation $k\in\{3,5,8\}$; neuron-ablation $k\in\{256,512,1024\}$; steering $\alpha\in\{0.5,1.0,1.5\}$; and directional ablation. Within each model-dataset pair, conditions share prompts, judges, and model-specific length limits. Intervention strength is varied using a dose-sweep. Uncertainty intervals use paired bootstrap over prompt IDs, preserving the shared prompts across conditions.


\subsection{Judges and their validation}
\label{sec:judges}
A four-class gibberish detector defines the coherence filter. Qwen3-30B is used to label answer harmfulness and refusal, predict harmfulness from the trace alone, and mark sentence-level safety reasoning. A Qwen3-14B LoRA judge supplies the pathway labels used for process level interpretation. Prompts, parsers, and full-per-label results are described in detail in Appendix ~\ref{sec:appendix-judges}. 

Two annotators independently completed a blind set of 140 unique tasks (80 harmful/not final answer, 40 trace only predictions, and 20 sentence level annotations). Table~\ref{tab:judge-val} reports the load bearing decisions; the response-harmfulness and trace monitor judges perform approximately at the observed human-human reliability ceiling.
