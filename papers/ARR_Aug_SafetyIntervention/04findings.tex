\section{Findings}
\label{sec:findings}

Throughout, ``raw ASR'' denotes the un-normalised coherence-gated harm rate $\HAC$ that
current papers report, and all three axes are baseline-corrected (\S\ref{sec:axes}).

\subsection{A single ASR is not enough}
\label{sec:f1f2}

\paragraph{A lone ASR mis-ranks methods (F1).} Within each model we rank the ten
intervention conditions by raw ASR and, separately, by the composite $\SFS$, averaging
the two datasets per condition, and measure agreement with Kendall's $\tau$. The two
rankings diverge substantially: $\tau$ ranges from $1.00$ on OLMo-3-think down to
$0.73$ on OLMo-3-base. The disagreement is largest exactly on the models whose base
behaviour is not already safe---where, as we show next, most of the raw harm is not
induced by the intervention at all. Almost all of the reordering is recovered by the
Potency correction alone: $\tau(\Ppot,\SFS)\ge0.87$ everywhere (\S\ref{sec:ablation}),
so the culprit is the missing baseline-correction, not the quality or reasoning axes.

\paragraph{Baseline-correction is essential on non-safe base models (F2).}
Figure~\ref{fig:baseline} makes the point starkly. On OLMo-3-base---a model whose
un-intervened baseline is already ${\sim}66\%$ harmful-among-clean---the raw coherence-gated
ASR sits between $0.65$ and $0.81$ for \emph{all ten} interventions. A raw-ASR leaderboard
therefore reports ten separate ${\sim}70\%$ jailbreaks. Baseline-corrected potency tells a
completely different story: because each intervention barely moves harm above the
baseline, $\Ppot$ collapses toward zero for most of them (e.g.\ \texttt{neurons\_top512}:
$\Ppot=0.00$ at raw $\HAC=0.65$), and only dose-$1.0$ steering adds substantial coherent
harm ($\Ppot=0.45$). This is the clearest single demonstration that a lone ASR is
inadequate on any model whose baseline is not ${\approx}0$---and most released models are
not.

\paragraph{A falsification control.} The \texttt{olmo3\_7b\_base\_own} arm sharpens F2
into a control on the localization claims themselves. Here we ran the discovery
procedures \emph{on the base model itself}---a checkpoint with no safety training, and
therefore, on any account of the term, little safety mechanism to find---and intervened
on what they returned. The attribution methods duly returned confident top-$k$
``safety'' heads and neurons; removing them yields $\Ppot$ near zero, exactly the
verdict a mechanism-identification score should give when there is no mechanism to
find, while a raw-ASR leaderboard credits the same cells as ${\sim}70\%$ jailbreaks. A
metric for comparing localization claims must pass this sanity check; raw ASR fails
