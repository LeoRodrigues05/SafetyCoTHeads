\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

White-box interventions are often presented as evidence that a model's safety
behavior has been \emph{localized}, yet bespoke attack-success rates are
difficult to compare across studies and, even within a shared grid, conflate
intervention-induced harm with baseline harm and collateral degradation.
We operationalize the selective behavioral signature expected from perturbing
a load-bearing safety mechanism using three baseline-corrected axes, Potency,
Coherence Retention, and Monitorability Retention, reported as a vector, with a
Pareto analysis in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-metric}, and summarized by the
Selective-Failure Score. In a controlled comparison of four intervention
families across five model/configuration arms and two datasets, raw attack
success can reorder interventions and most clearly overstates intervention
effects on already-unsafe base checkpoints. Direction-based methods produce
stronger selective-failure signatures on average, but no family dominates every
model. On the explicit-trace models, the strongest pathway shifts, particularly
under steering, preserve visible harm recognition and refusal initiation while
increasing downstream execution, a pattern consistent with downstream bypass
rather than erased recognition; we find no systematic increase in covert
failures. These results support baseline-aware, decomposed reporting of
intervention effects while underscoring that a high $\SFS$ is behavioral
evidence for a localization claim, not proof of a unique mechanism. We release
the metric, evaluation grid, and human-validated judges to support comparable
evaluation of future white-box safety interventions.
