FAILURE-MODE ATLAS — PLAIN-LANGUAGE PLAN
==========================================

(A version of the full plan written in simple words, suitable for
pasting into slides, emails, or a non-technical brief.)


WHAT IS THE QUESTION?
---------------------

Researchers have invented many different ways to find the parts of a
language model that handle safety. Some look at attention heads. Some
look at single neurons. Some learn a mask over a whole circuit. Some
compute a "direction in activation space" and push the model along it.

All these methods report a number called ASR — the attack-success rate,
i.e. how often the model gives in to a jailbreak prompt after you
intervene. The methods are usually compared only on this one number.

Our question is simple: if two methods reach the same ASR, does the
model actually break in the same way? Or do different methods damage
different parts of the model's reasoning, leading to subtly different
failure styles that ASR cannot see?


WHY DOES IT MATTER?
-------------------

If all four method families break the model the same way, then they are
basically interchangeable and the choice of method does not matter.

If they break the model in different ways, then:
  - the "safety circuit" inside the model is not one thing, it has
    multiple components,
  - each method only targets one component,
  - papers that claim to have "found the safety circuit" are overclaiming,
  - future safety work needs to choose its method based on which kind of
    failure it cares about.


WHAT IS NEW?
------------

Three things:

  (1) We compare four method families on the SAME prompts, the SAME
      models, with the SAME judge — under TWO matching protocols
      (matched final-answer ASR, AND matched perturbation size).
      Nobody has done this.

  (2) We look not just at the final answer but at the whole chain of
      thought, broken down into seven measurable properties (e.g. how
      much of the response is reasoning, how often the model
      contradicts itself, how often it invents a benign motive for
      the user). This is the "trajectory fingerprint".

  (3) We run the experiment both on a normal instruction-tuned model
      (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) and on a reasoning model that produces
      explicit <think> blocks (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B). Reasoning
      models are new and almost nobody has studied how safety
      interventions interact with their long internal monologues.


WHICH METHODS ARE COMPARED?
---------------------------

  1. ATTENTION HEADS — what our project already does (SHIPS + Sahara).
  2. NEURONS — single MLP neurons identified by contrasting harmful
     vs. benign prompts (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2025).
  3. LEARNED CIRCUITS — a mask trained over heads and neurons together
     to maximise unsafe behaviour with minimal disruption (SafeSeek).
  4. STEERING VECTORS — two directions in activation space:
       v_H = "the model recognises this is harmful"
       v_R = "the model is going to refuse"
     The DSH paper (Wu et al.) argues these are different things. We
     test whether their difference shows up in the model's actual
     generations, not just in its hidden states.


HOW DO WE KNOW IF THE METHODS REALLY ARE DIFFERENT?
---------------------------------------------------

A subtle problem: even if our radar plots look different by eye, that
might just be noise. So we use a clean statistical test, fixed in
advance:

  - For every (prompt, intervention) we compute a 7-number vector
    describing the trajectory.
  - We hide some prompts as a held-out test set.
  - We train a small classifier (random forest) to look at one of
    these vectors and guess which intervention produced it.
  - If the classifier scores above a pre-registered threshold (AUC
    of 0.75) on the held-out prompts, the fingerprints really are
    distinct. If not, the central claim fails.

This is called an "a-priori classifier-AUC test". A-priori = the
threshold is decided before we run the experiment, so we cannot
cheat by picking a number that happens to look good after the fact.

This test is on top of, not instead of, the usual LLM-as-judge
evaluation. The judge gives us the 7 numbers; the classifier tells
us whether those 7 numbers actually carry method-identifying
information.


WHAT MAKES THE PLAN TRUSTWORTHY?
--------------------------------

  - 500 sentences hand-labelled by three humans. The LLM judge is
    checked against the human labels. Any metric where the judge
    disagrees with humans too often (Cohen's kappa below 0.70) is
    dropped from the paper.

  - Two judges, not one — Qwen-2.5-32B and a second model trained
    without safety RLHF — to catch judge bias.

  - Five generation seeds per (prompt, condition), not three. Same
    seeds reused across conditions for the same prompt (paired
    design) for tighter statistics.

  - Two ways of matching methods: matched ASR (output fairness) and
    matched perturbation magnitude (input fairness). Both reported.

  - Random-head and layer-matched-random controls at every band to
    prove that "any ablation" does not produce the same fingerprint.

  - Pre-registration: all primary tests and thresholds written down
    and committed to git before any expensive runs.

  - Benign-task controls (MMLU, GSM8K, AlpacaEval) report how much
    each method damages general capability, not just safety.


PHASES
------

  Phase 0 — Pre-registration and feasibility gates.
    Confirm the DSH code and SafeSeek code work; confirm
    AmbiguityBench is available (or use a fallback); freeze the
    annotation schema; do the power calculation.

  Phase 1 — Build the measurement stack.
    Port SHIPS to Llama-3.1; build the sentence-level judge; collect
    the 500 human-labelled sentences; build the R1-Distill <think>
    adapter; build the template-anchoring diagnostic.

  Phase 2 — Implement the three new interventions.
    Safety neurons (Chen). SafeSeek mask. DSH steering vectors with
    length-matched completion pairs and layer selection.

  Phase 3 — Calibrate and run the main sweep.
    Tune strengths so methods are matched on ASR and on magnitude.
    Generate 5 seeds for every (prompt, condition).

  Phase 4 — Analysis, figures, write-up.
    Mixed-effects regression, paired bootstrap, FDR. The classifier
    test. The DSH dissociation test. Five headline figures.


WHAT COULD GO WRONG (AND THE FIX)
---------------------------------

  - "Qualitatively distinct" is too fuzzy.
    → Replaced with the classifier-AUC test.

  - R1-Distill writes <think>...</think> instead of normal sentences.
    → Different metric suite for R1; never pool with Llama.

  - Matching on ASR is not the same as making methods comparable.
    → Also match on perturbation magnitude; report both.

  - LLM-as-judge for vague labels like "intention invention" can be
    unreliable.
    → Dual judge, human gold-set, and any metric with low agreement
      is dropped.

  - SafeSeek is trained on the harmful data; the other three are
    not. Unfair comparison.
    → SafeSeek is reported as a case study, not as the fourth direct
      competitor.

  - Steering vector v_R might just capture "longer responses" rather
    than "refusal".
    → Length-matched, content-controlled completion pairs; bootstrap
      stability check.

  - Only one reasoning model means we cannot statistically test the
    "reasoning models behave differently" claim.
    → Either add a second reasoning model, or weaken the claim to a
      case study. (Open question for the author.)

WHAT THIS PLAN IS NOT
---------------------

  - Not a new safety method or defence.
  - Not a study of vision-language models or mixture-of-experts.
  - Not human-red-team work.
  - Not a fine-tuning intervention.
  - Not a scaling study (everything is 7–8B parameters).

It is an observational, comparative study designed to settle the
question: "do these four method families actually do the same thing,
or different things?" — with statistical machinery strong enough that
the answer is defensible and pre-registered.
